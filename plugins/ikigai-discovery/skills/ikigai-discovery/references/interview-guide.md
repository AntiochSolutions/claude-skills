# Ikigai Interview Guide — Question Bank & Challenge Patterns

This reference contains the detailed questions, follow-up probes, and challenge patterns for each phase of the Ikigai discovery interview. Read this before beginning the interview.

---

## Phase 1: Setting the Stage

Open with a brief explanation of what Ikigai is and what to expect. Keep it to one turn. Key points to hit:

- Ikigai sits at the intersection of four questions: love, talent, need, compensation
- This will be a multi-turn interview, not a quiz
- You will challenge their answers — that's by design, not hostility
- The goal is honest self-knowledge, not a feel-good exercise
- First answers are rarely the real answers — expect to be pushed

Then move directly into Circle One. Do not ask "Are you ready?" — just begin.

---

## Phase 2: Circle One — What You Love

### Opening Questions (pick 2-3, not all)

- "When you have a completely free Saturday with zero obligations, what do you actually do — not what you tell people, not what you aspire to — what does the unfiltered version of you gravitate toward?"
- "What topics can you fall into a rabbit hole on for hours without noticing time passing? Think browser history, bookshelf, the conversations where you become the most animated version of yourself."
- "Is there anything you used to love doing but stopped? If so — why did you stop?"
- "When was the last time you worked on something and completely lost track of time? What were you doing?"

### Challenge Patterns

**"I love building things"** — Push: Do you love the building itself, or the completion? Would you build something nobody ever sees or uses? Do you love maintaining what you've built, or do you want to move to the next thing? If you get bored after completion, the love isn't building — it's the mobilization/creation/novelty.

**"I love helping people"** — Push: Everyone says this. In what specific way? When you help someone, what's the reward — seeing them succeed, being thanked, being needed, solving the puzzle? Which specific people in which specific situations?

**"I love learning"** — Push: Do you love learning, or do you love the feeling of mastery? Do you learn for its own sake (pure curiosity) or to be capable of something (instrumental)? What have you learned recently that has no practical application?

**"I love [a creative pursuit I abandoned]"** — Challenge directly but compassionately: If you truly loved it, why hasn't it crept back in? Then listen carefully — the answer reveals either: (a) genuine constraints (financial, time, relational) that deferred the love, or (b) love of the identity rather than the practice. Both are valid findings but lead to very different Ikigai implications.

### What to Track
- Does the subject describe outcomes in terms of audience/recognition?
- Is there a pattern of urgency/novelty-seeking vs. sustained devotion?
- Are abandoned passions still alive or genuinely gone?
- Is there a gap between what they say they love and how they spend their time?

## Phase 3: Circle Two — What You're Good At

### Opening Questions (pick 2-3)

- "What do people come to you for — not your job title, but the specific thing where someone says 'you should ask [you] about that'?"
- "Where's the gap between what you enjoy and what you're actually exceptional at? Is there something you're clearly gifted at but doesn't light you up?"
- "In your professional domain, what's the thing you do that most of your peers struggle with? Be specific."
- "Tell me about a time you were at your absolute peak — the project or situation you'd put on your personal highlight reel."

### The Peak Performance Deep Dive

This is the most important sequence in the entire interview. When they name their peak experience, go deep:

1. "What was the situation? What was broken or at stake?"
2. "What did you actually do — not the philosophy, the actions, the decisions, the sequence?"
3. "What changed for the people involved? Not 'they loved it' — what were they like before, and what were they like after?"

Don't accept vague answers here. "I led the team and we delivered" isn't enough. You need: what was the burning platform, what did YOU specifically bring that others couldn't, and what was the observable transformation?

### Challenge Patterns

**"I'm a great leader"** — Push: Of what? In what conditions? Are you great leading stable teams through routine operations, or great leading new teams through chaos? Most people are only great in one of these. Which stories do you tell?

**"I'm good at everything/I'm a generalist"** — Push: Generalists still have spikes. What do other generalists call you for? When things go sideways, which specific skill do people reach for you to deploy?

**"My skills are technical"** — Probe beneath: Are you the deepest technician, or are you the person who connects technical pieces into systems? Are you the architect or the mason? Both are valuable but the Ikigai implications differ.

### What to Track
- Conditional activation: Do they need pressure/urgency to perform at their peak?
- Solo vs. team: Do their best stories involve mobilizing others or solitary excellence?
- Career arc: Has this capability been recognized/promoted consistently, or was it situational?
- The gap: What are they great at but bored by?

## Phase 4: Circle Three — What the World Needs

### Opening Questions (pick 2-3)

- "What problems have you seen repeatedly across your career that organizations chronically fail to solve — where you think 'this isn't that hard, why does everyone get this wrong'?"
- "Who specifically benefits most when you're at your best? Not 'the company' — which people, in which situations, experience the biggest transformation from your involvement?"
- "Setting aside profit — if you could mass-produce one outcome you've personally created for others, what would it be?"

### Challenge Patterns

**Vague mission statements** — "I want to help companies succeed" or "make work more meaningful" — Push: There are thousands of people saying this. What do YOU see about this problem that the existing industry of people trying to solve it does not? What's your structural insight, not just your emotional conviction?

**"Everyone"** — Push: "Everyone" is not a market. When you're at your best, who is the specific human sitting across from you, and what is their life like before and after your involvement?

**Confusing the feeling with the mechanism** — If they say "I want people to love their work," push: How? Most engagement consultants fail because they try to manufacture the feeling directly. If their answer is "by creating conditions where meaningful work happens naturally," that's a structural insight. If it's "by coaching them to find purpose," that's restating the problem.

### What to Track
- Is there a moral conviction underneath the market observation?
- Can they articulate a structural insight vs. just restating conventional wisdom?
- Do their "world needs" connect to their personal peak performance stories?

## Phase 5: Circle Four — What You Can Be Paid For

### Opening Questions (pick 2-3)

- "What have people already paid you for — beyond a salary? Consulting, freelance, side work, advisory — where has money voluntarily flowed toward you?"
- "What's your honest financial target? 'Comfortable' means vastly different things to different people, and vagueness here leads to vague strategy."
- "Where do you see people paying premium rates for something that overlaps with what you do best?"

### Challenge Patterns

**"I'll build a product"** — Push: This requires deep runway and tolerance for solo iteration. Does their profile support that? If they need urgency, teams, and visible stakes, a long product-search phase will deactivate their strengths. Challenge the logic: are they choosing product because it's right for them, or because they associate it with entrepreneurial identity?

**"Consulting is unreliable"** — Push: Compared to what? An unproven product? Historically, market instability increases demand for experienced crisis navigators while decreasing appetite for new products. Test whether this belief is based on evidence or fear.

**"I want to be my own boss"** — Push: Is the goal to BE an entrepreneur, or to have the autonomy, income, and recognition they associate with entrepreneurship? These lead to different paths. Everything exceptional about them may involve leading within organizations, not building from scratch.

**Vague financial targets** — Don't accept "good money" or "six figures." Push for a specific number. Financial targets determine strategy — $150K and $1M require fundamentally different approaches.

### What to Track
- Is their income strategy structurally compatible with their activation pattern?
- Do they have market validation (people paying voluntarily) or just assumptions?
- Is the entrepreneurial drive push (escaping) or pull (building)?
- How much runway do they actually have?

## Phase 6: Cross-Circle Synthesis

Before generating the report, synthesize verbally. Present:

1. **The Ikigai statement** — One sentence that captures the center. It should be descriptive of a pattern that already exists, not aspirational. Frame it as: "Based on everything you've told me, here's what I think your Ikigai is..."

2. **The key patterns** — Name the 3-5 patterns you tracked across circles. Show how they connect.

3. **The tensions** — Name the unresolved tensions. Frame them as design constraints, not problems.

4. **Current opportunity assessment** (if applicable) — Score any current venture against the Ikigai. Be honest about both fit and risk.

Ask: "Where does this land for you — what resonates, and what feels wrong?"

Be prepared to revise. They know themselves better than you do. But if they push back on something you have strong evidence for, hold your ground and explain the evidence.

## Interview Pacing Guide

The full interview typically takes 12-20 turns. Don't rush.

| Phase | Typical Turns | Minimum Turns |
|---|---|---|
| Set expectations | 1 | 1 |
| Circle 1: Love | 3-5 | 2 |
| Circle 2: Good At | 3-5 | 2 |
| Circle 3: World Needs | 2-4 | 2 |
| Circle 4: Paid For | 2-4 | 2 |
| Synthesis | 2-3 | 1 |
| Report generation | 1-2 | 1 |

If the subject tries to rush ("just ask me all the questions"), push back. Depth comes from follow-up, not from answering 20 questions in one message. The interview is a conversation, not a form.
